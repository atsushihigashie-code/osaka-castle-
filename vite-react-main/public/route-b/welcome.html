Before You Begin

Two Castles, One Ground

What you're about to walk through isn't one castle's story — it's two. And the second one was built directly on top of the ashes of the first.

1500
Ishiyama Hongan-ji, a fortified temple, is established on this ground.
1580
Oda Nobunaga wins the Battle of Ishiyama, clearing the site.
1583
Toyotomi Hideyoshi begins building his castle on the ruins of the temple.
1615
The Toyotomi castle falls in the Summer Siege of Osaka — burned to the ground.
1620
The Tokugawa Shogunate spends 10 years building an entirely new castle — a different shape, a different era.
1665
Lightning burns down the Tokugawa keep. Only its stone base survives.
1945
WWII air raids badly damage the surviving turrets and keep base.
1997
A full restoration is completed — the castle you'll walk through today.
Two Clans, One Battlefield

The last battle of the Sengoku period

On one side, the Toyotomi — Hideyoshi's widow Yodo-dono and his son Hideyori, defending everything their family had built. On the other, the Tokugawa clan, determined to end them for good. In the summer of 1615, that conflict reached this exact ground — and when the smoke cleared, an entire era of Japanese history had ended with it.

The Castle Beneath the Castle

Hideyoshi's castle never left. It's still down there.

After the siege, the Tokugawa didn't just rebuild — they buried Hideyoshi's entire castle under a fresh layer of earth, several meters deep, and raised their own castle on top of it. Every stone wall you'll see today sits on that hidden layer. Somewhere beneath your feet, the original Osaka Castle is still there.
